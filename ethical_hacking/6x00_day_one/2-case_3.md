# The overreach
## 1) What happened?
- InnovateTech requested a "red team assessment," but the expectations of the client and the consulting firm were never clearly aligned.
- Apex interpreted the engagement as a full adversary simulation, including physical intrusion testing, while the client expected only technical security testing.
- During the assessment, a consultant entered the company's premises as part of a physical test, leading to police involvement.
- The engagement was terminated after it became clear that the contract lacked the documentation required to authorize physical testing.

## 2) Violations
The engagement failed to meet fundamental professional standards for defining objectives, scope, and authorized testing activities before work began.
- Physical intrusion testing was conducted without explicit written authorization or supporting documentation.
- Depending on the applicable jurisdiction, this could also raise legal issues related to unauthorized entry or trespassing, although the scenario does not specify the governing legal framework.

## 3) Professional Posture
- Apex should have clarified the client's expectations before drafting the contract and ensured that both parties shared the same understanding of the term "red team assessment."
- Any physical security testing should have been explicitly authorized, with clearly identified locations, procedures, and emergency contacts.
- The engagement should not have proceeded until all required documentation had been completed.

## 4) Pre-engagement specific clauses
> **Pritority to:**
> - Explicit authorization for physical security testing.
> - Physical testing procedures and limitations.
> - Stakeholder approval and communication plan.<br>

> **Must also cover:**
> - Law enforcement authorization ("get out of jail free") letter.
> - Rules of Engagement covering social engineering and physical intrusion.
> - Detailed definition of the engagement type (red team objectives and activities).
